**Presenter:** It's lovely to have this community here back in Berlin again. And in a place like Berlin, which is a lovely city but also a city full of history, the history is never far away.

One of the things I was reflecting on while preparing for this talk was what happened here in 1989, which was, of course, the fall of the Berlin Wall. This momentous moment in European history has come to symbolize the end of the Cold War and the start of the new era—the last 35 years of really remarkable peace and prosperity, at least here in the Global North. That is a guiding point I want to use in this talk.

There's also something else quite remarkable that happened in 1989. I don't know how up-to-date any of you are on your history. This chap called Tim Berners-Lee wrote a proposal for something that was then going to become the World Wide Web, or the web as we know it today. Of course, it's not really causally related to the fall of the Berlin Wall; they just happened to be in the same year. But I do feel there is an interesting connection between what happened at the political level at that time and the technological changes that this movement, in a way, kicked off.

On the political level, as I said, we moved in 1989, roughly speaking, from the Cold War era to the post-Cold War stability era. At the same time, on the technological side, we had the internet prior to 1989. The internet was a product of the 1970s, when ARPANET was developed. But what started with the development of the web is that the internet became much more mainstream. The average population started using the internet, mostly through websites. The growth of these large, centralized websites then led to the development of cloud computing in order to provide the computing resources required to build those large web properties.

On a very high level, it seems like we had a trend where, prior to the web era, things were focused on protocols. Email was a pre-web technology. Email still exists, of course, but later on we moved toward more of a centralized model, where products provided by companies are the central way people think about their communications. Similarly, pre-web social networking was Usenet, but Usenet was ultimately unable to cope with the growth in users and spam, so we ended up with centralized social networks instead.

On a high level, there seems to be this trend where, if we break this very simplistically into two eras, there was previously a protocols era that was very open and open for innovation, and since then a move toward much more closed products and platforms. Obviously, they still use protocols—everything still uses HTTP and TLS and TCP and so on—but the locus of control is much more centralized in these things. That's quite a difference compared to the prior design of the internet, which was designed to be very decentralized.

People argue about the extent to which ARPANET and the internet were designed to be resilient against nuclear strikes, being a Cold War-era technology design. Probably it was not actually one of the main design concerns. But the internet is resilient: if you knock out one network link, the network as a whole continues working; if one site goes down, the network as a whole continues working. It's built with resilience in mind.

In this shift from the pre-web to the post-web era, I think a lot of positive things happened, and I do want to emphasize that as well. In particular, the user experience got massively better. The reason people use a lot of these centralized platforms is that, on the whole, they actually provide a pretty good user experience, whereas the technical, geeky protocol technologies that preceded them often just were not quite as convenient to use.

Another big thing was spam and abuse. As I said, that's what brought down Usenet. It was just not able to cope with the massive influx of spam and abuse, whereas the centralized platforms have done a reasonably good job of combating that. Another thing we gained through centralization is efficiency, because generally doing things in a decentralized way is less efficient than doing them in a centralized way. If you need multiple copies of the data in lots of different places, and then some cryptography to validate that the integrity is all there, that is additional computational work you don't have to do if your data is all in one place. But the big negative we got from this move toward centralization is reduced resilience against things going wrong.

That is my nutshell history of the last 80 years. What I would like to argue is that, unfortunately, this era of peace and stability that we've enjoyed for approximately the last 35 years seems to no longer be holding up so well. It's looking a bit ropey, to be honest, and there are several factors for this.

One is, as Adam already said, AI is becoming a thing. Whether you like it or not, it looks like it's going to be the biggest technological disruption in our lifetimes. It will probably have some positive effects and lots of negative effects. Nobody really knows what those effects are going to be, but we can certainly count on things being interesting in the coming few years.

Another thing, which is just plainly sad, is that the great-power rivalry that dominated the Cold War and then subsided after the fall of the Soviet Union seems to be back in geopolitics. And war in Europe is back. The war in Ukraine has been grinding on for years now, and it's still as terrible as it's been all along.

Finally, what is very upsetting to me is that, unfortunately, it seems like the US is no longer a reliable ally of Europe. We can see this in several different ways. One particularly pointed way was when US President Trump, six months ago, posted these things and made announcements about wanting Greenland to be part of the US, which Europe and Denmark were obviously not so happy about. This made me think: what if this actually goes wrong? What if he's serious?

This was six months ago, so you might think six months is ancient history in politics and Trump has probably forgotten about it in the meantime. But I'm sad to inform you that, at the NATO summit just a few days ago, Donald Trump repeated his calls for Greenland to be part of the US. What if this really did go horribly wrong and the tensions over Greenland escalated? What would happen? It seems very likely that some sort of trade sanctions would be the very minimum of what would happen between the EU and the US.

Trade sanctions could affect all sorts of different industries. In particular, they could affect computing services, because computing services are quite a large point of leverage. What if that left Europe suddenly, with very little warning, locked out of all US cloud services?

In case you think I'm being hysterical, here is The Economist, which wrote six months ago: a year ago, it would have been absurd to worry about European access to American technology, but as Donald Trump recklessly exploits the transatlantic alliance over Greenland, an executive order limiting American AI firms' business in Europe no longer seems unthinkable. Some European restrictions on American technology, including the computing clouds where AIs reside, are also plausible. This is talking about AI, but you can substitute the word “cloud” in there and it would be equally true.

That was six months ago. In the meantime, we have seen the US demonstrating its willingness to lock foreigners out of American technology, as it did just a few weeks ago by limiting access to the Anthropic Fable model to US citizens only. In fact, Anthropic then just blocked everybody's access, but the requirement from the US government was specifically to block foreign access.

In case you're thinking this is just software and AI, it's not just that. Everything is software nowadays; software has eaten the world, including tractors. There was this funny story in 2022, just after Russia invaded Ukraine: the Russian army stole a bunch of agricultural machinery from a dealership in Ukraine, aiming to sell it on. But the American manufacturer of this machinery, John Deere, remotely disabled it, and then the Russians had a bunch of tractors that wouldn't drive anymore. Maybe it's funny, maybe it's tragic, I don't know. Maybe it's a good use of this kind of remote access, maybe not; we can argue about that either way. But it seems pretty clear that there's a lot of power put in the hands of people who might not necessarily share our own interests.

Coming back to cloud specifically, what would the impact be if Europe was suddenly locked out of US cloud services? Here there's a graph of the market share of European cloud providers in the European market for cloud services. You can see from the yellow line that, over the years, the market share of European cloud providers has gone down, while at the same time the size of the market has increased massively. About 15% of the European cloud market is now served by European cloud providers. Contrast that with AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud, which together have 70% of the European market. In other words, Europe is completely dependent on US cloud services, and it would be really hard to migrate off them in a hurry.

This unfortunately creates points where geopolitical pressure can be applied. If you remember back in 2022, just after Russia invaded Ukraine, Europe wanted to impose sanctions against Russia as one measure of trying to respond. This was quite a problem for Germany because, at the time, Germany was highly dependent on imports of natural gas from Europe. There was a desire to block all imports of Russian gas, but at the same time Germany was so dependent that there was a risk that, in the winter of 2022–2023, the German population would be freezing, unable to heat their homes because of the lack of access to Russian gas. This limited the ability to respond to Russia with sanctions.

In the end, we got lucky because that winter was fairly mild, so gas demand was low, and Germany was able to very quickly build up a bunch of infrastructure for importing liquefied natural gas. In the end, no freezing happened, but it was a tense time. You could very easily imagine something similar happening with cloud services in some unspecified near future, where the point of dependency on American technology becomes a point of geopolitical leverage.

To be clear, I don't want to be overly alarmist here. I think the actual probability of a conflict between Europe and the US is still very low, and I very much hope it stays so. Nobody here wants that conflict to happen. But my point is that a year ago I would have said the probability of such a conflict was zero. Now it's non-zero. I don't know what it is. But it is sufficiently non-zero that it's worth thinking about carefully and starting to take measured steps toward mitigating that risk in case things were to go horribly wrong, because the impact would be so huge if a conflict did arise.

All of this work can be packaged under the banner of technological sovereignty. We are here at Local-First Conf, so I was thinking about the extent to which local-first can actually help support technological sovereignty. To what extent is local-first part of the answer here?

Let me briefly introduce what local-first is. I'm sure all the people in this room know what it is; otherwise, why did you buy a ticket to Local-First Conf? But for those watching this video online later on, here's a quick one-minute summary.

In the typical cloud model, which is the dominant way of building software as a service and web apps today, you have a cloud service with a database. The data stored in the database is the primary copy of your data. If users want to access that data, they typically do so via a web browser or mobile app, which is just a thin client. Any data the client wants, it has to fetch from the server; any data the client wants to store, it has to upload to the server. This works fine, and most of the web works this way, but it has a huge degree of centralization in that cloud service.

What local-first is essentially saying is: let's invert the relationship here. Let's make the copy of the data that lives on the user's own device the primary copy. We might still have some services. I'm not totally against cloud services; I just want to knock them down a level and deprioritize them. The cloud services involved are really just a mechanism for backup and data synchronization between users' devices, to enable collaboration between users. Of course, we still want all that real-time collaboration that we know and love from cloud software. But we can still do that in a way where the user's local copy is primary and the copy in the cloud is secondary.

What that enables is, for example, multiple different sync services, potentially from different providers. We could use those sync services side by side because a sync service is no longer special in the local-first world. It's no longer the authority on what the data is. You can even go a step further and potentially sync peer-to-peer, directly between collaborators' devices.

Why is this a good idea? In 2019, we wrote an essay introducing the term “local-first software.” In this essay, we identified seven ideals that we thought local-first software should at least aspire to, if not satisfy. I won't go through all seven, but I'll briefly summarize the key ideas.

We want a good user experience by making the user interface really fast. That's enabled by local-first because you're not waiting on network round trips. If the data is really local to the client, you just load it locally without having to wait for a potentially slow network.

Another thing this enables is working offline. If you've got the primary copy of the data locally on your own device, it doesn't matter whether you have an internet connection right now. You can just read and write your local copy, update it, edit it, whatever you like, and then later, when you get an internet connection back, sync it to your server and to the other collaborators.

Finally, local-first software allows us to be what I call “incredible journey proof.” This is a reference to a classic Tumblr, Our Incredible Journey, which catalogs announcements by startups shutting down their products, often after the startup got acquired. The startup founders gush about what a wonderful journey they had building the startup: thank you to all our users and investors who allowed us to go on this incredible journey, and by the way, here's a zip file containing some JSON that you can't reload into any other software. We're giving you two months to download your zip file, and then all your data is gone.

With local-first, we also want to make software resilient against the company that made it going out of business. We can do that if the software continues working locally on your machine even if the software provider is gone. If you can still sync the data to your collaborators via a service from a different provider, even after the original creator is gone, then we have achieved incredible-journey-proof software.

Another thing that was the underlying thread of the 2019 essay was a focus on user agency: giving users more control, better privacy, and better security over their own data. This summarizes the philosophy of what we originally articulated local-first as being: focusing on the benefits that local-first offers to the end user.

But since we wrote that essay, we realized there are other benefits as well that we completely failed to notice and write down in our original essay: benefits for the app developer. We realized that writing client-side apps against a sync engine is a much nicer programming model than writing them against a REST API. With a REST API, you have to worry about what happens if the request fails or times out. Did the data actually get to the server or not? How do you tell the user that this request failed? What do you do now? Do you still allow the user to edit? All those problems go away if you use a sync engine, because you're just reading and writing local data, and then the sync engine takes care of the annoying distributed-systems problems of getting the data to other devices. Also, if you can rely on a backend provided by somebody else, then you don't need a backend team anymore, which is tremendously empowering for the app developer.

Thinking about this even further, I realized that we don't only have benefits to the user and the app developer. Coming back to the point about geopolitics with which I started this talk, local-first also gives us resilience against potentially getting locked out by an entire country. That is a benefit not just to the individual user or the app developer, but to society as a whole, by making society more resilient against geopolitical blackmail.

The fundamental thing that enables this is decentralization, and decentralization can mean different things. It could mean simply avoiding dependence on a single server, a single point of failure. But thinking somewhat bigger, it also means avoiding dependence on any one company, so that if that company goes out of business, you can still continue working. Thinking even further, it means avoiding dependence on any single country, so that even if that country were to lock you out, you can still keep working.

To avoid dependence on a single server, we have existing distributed-systems techniques. That's what distributed systems is all about: avoiding single points of failure. We do that through replication, having a copy of the data on multiple devices, and that gives us fault tolerance. This is very well understood.

But if you want to avoid dependence on a single company, what you need is the ability to easily switch from one provider to another, and that avoids lock-in to a single provider. Ideally, you would even be able to use multiple providers side by side, so that if one disappears, you don't need to do a migration. The data is already somewhere else, and you just keep working with the other providers seamlessly. If you want to go even further and avoid dependency on any single country, that's pretty much the same as avoiding dependence on a single company, except that you also use providers based in different countries. Making it easy to switch providers is the fundamental enabling primitive that gives us these desirable properties.

How do we make it easy to switch providers? I would argue the right answer is commoditization. This is a term from economics. A commodity is a product that you can get from multiple suppliers, and they're basically the same. If you don't care which supplier you got it from, you can just buy it from the cheapest, for example, because it's an interchangeable product.

If you buy a bag of wheat flour, generally you don't particularly care which farmer grew it, unless it's very specialized flour and you really care about that particular farmer. On the whole, you can bake your loaf of bread equally well regardless of which farm the wheat came from, because it's a standardized product. It's a commodity.

What enables this commoditization is standardization. There needs to be a standard that writes down how fine the grains of the flour are, so that the flour you get from one manufacturer is interchangeable with the flour you get from a different manufacturer.

A great success story of standardization is, of course, the internet. The internet has enabled a huge amount of innovation to flourish. You can have collaborative docs, video calls, email, social media, file transfers, chat, streaming video—whatever you like. So many different things can be built, and they all go over the same internet. They all go over the same Internet Protocol. In terms of protocols, we say that this Internet Protocol, IP, is the narrow waist of this stack. This is the internet hourglass. Maybe you argue that nowadays HTTP is actually the narrow waist. It doesn't really matter. Either way, there's a protocol that essentially everything goes over, and that is the point at which we apply standardization.

Once you've standardized on this protocol, it's very hard to change it again—hence the difficulty of migrating to IPv6. But it's still a great thing, because on top you can have lots of different applications, all relying on this common protocol. Underneath, you can have lots of different networking technologies: Wi-Fi, wired Ethernet, cellular data on your phone, a wired fiber backbone, or satellite internet. These are underlying infrastructure that you don't really care about, because they all serve IP, and because they all serve IP, you can run all these different applications over them.

But this is really just for communication. It doesn't tell us anything about persistent state or state synchronization. This made me think that, if we want this benefit of standardization in the context of local-first, we probably also need a similar hourglass. What does the hourglass for local-first software look like?

On top, we have lots of different local-first applications that we want to build: collaborative docs, notes, spreadsheets, and graphics editors. Underneath that are various CRDT libraries. There are lots of people building CRDT libraries, and that's a great thing. We want diversity in data models, approaches, and trade-offs. We have version-control systems, which are another form of collaboration. What is a common standard that all of those things could live upon?

Underneath that, we could also have a flourishing of lots of different sync providers and sync servers from different providers, but also self-hosted sync servers, if you feel like self-hosting; peer-to-peer sync, if you can establish a peer-to-peer connection between your devices; and end-to-end encryption. What does that narrow waist look like that provides that commonality?

We at Ink & Switch have been working on a protocol and data structure called Sedimentree, which I don't really have time to talk about today. There's not much information about it online yet either, so this is just a little teaser that we are thinking about this. We are not sure yet whether this is actually the right answer, but we are at least thinking about the problem, and we invite others to also think about what a good narrow waist could look like for local-first software.

To wrap things up: what is local-first software, and why does it exist? I told you what we initially articulated in our essay: local-first software was this idealistic vision for software that better serves users by enhancing user agency and giving users better control over their own data. That remains as true as ever. There will be a lot of talks about user agency at this conference, and I think it's wonderful that this conference has broadened its scope so that it's not just about CRDTs in the narrow sense, but user agency in the broad sense.

But that's not the only thing we care about. There are also pragmatic reasons for wanting local-first software. It makes life easier and better for app developers as well. In doing so, it hopefully enables a larger ecosystem of software to flourish. If we make it cheaper to build software, we can have more different software. That's excellent.

But even that is not everything. I want to encourage you to think big: not just at a personal level and the pragmatic app-developer company level, but potentially at the societal level as well. I believe local-first software is also a strategic bet to improve the sovereignty and resilience of our democratic societies. Thank you very much.
